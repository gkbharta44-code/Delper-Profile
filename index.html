The Mission

Humanitarian by architecture, not by intention

Every commerce platform built before Delper has extracted value from the people at the base of the economic pyramid — charging them to transact, charging them to access, charging them to participate. Delper inverts this. The supply chain that has always taxed the consumer invisibly, the advertisers who have always profited from consumer attention, and the token traders who benefit from consumer-driven liquidity all pay into a single pool, returned as income to the person actually using the platform.

"The decision to charge the supply chain rather than the user is the founding humanitarian act. It cannot be removed without destroying the business model entirely — human welfare and commercial return are not in tension. They are the same variable."

Agent 6 · Precision Agronomy

Farm-to-city in 48 hours

An AI agronomy agent advises each of the 96 million partner farms per crop, per season, in 38 languages — coordinating harvest timing with logistics so staple food reaches resident kitchens within 48 hours of harvest.

Committed vs. Actual Cost

$37.44T committed · $4.20T–$4.51T spent

60% of Year 21 total platform revenue ($62.40T) is earmarked for food — $37.44T. Actual food cost is a fraction of that commitment, leaving a standing surplus (~$30.5T at Year 21) that funds programme resilience against price shocks.

Financial Inclusion

1.4 billion people, banked for the first time

People who have never held a bank account are issued one — not as a grant, but as the natural consequence of joining a platform that pays them to participate. No documentation barrier. No institutional relationship required.

Onboarding

Verified identity in 30 minutes

KYC screening recognises 11,000+ document types across 220+ countries. A Hyperledger-backed wallet with zero-knowledge-verified identity is issued the same day.

Cost to Serve

$28 to onboard · $4/year to maintain

Wallet issuance is a one-time $28 cost; ongoing annual operations run $4/wallet/year — a cost structure designed to make serving the world's poorest financially viable, not charitable.

Renewal, Not Abandonment

What happens to the city residents leave behind

When a population relocates to a completed smart city, the legacy city they leave is not abandoned. Delper reclaims and retrofits it — a second humanitarian commitment, run in one-for-one parity with the smart-city programme, so that renewal follows relocation everywhere it happens.

Funding the Commitment

$67.43T/yr at full 2,200-city scale

Retrofit yield is 10% per year on the $306.5B investment ($30.65B/city/yr) — financed via 25-year Medium Term Notes backed by completed smart-city collateral, the same funding logic that underwrites the resident benefit elsewhere in this programme.

No Medium Term Notes have been issued and no reclamation project has commenced. These figures are illustrative forward-looking projections.

Dignified Work

Employment, not just entitlement

For residents who work in a city's industries, the $247/month benefit arrives as part of an employment package rather than a standalone payment — the programme is designed to fund itself through productive work, not dependency.

Two figures here do not hold up against real-world benchmarks: 4.4 billion total jobs — 2M workers × 2,200 cities — is 122% of the entire global labour force (~3.6 billion people worldwide). Delper alone could not employ more people than exist in the global workforce, before counting every other job on Earth that still needs doing.

$4,000,000/worker/month in gross output (the basis for the $200,000/month net-revenue figure) implies $48,000,000 of individual annual output per worker — far beyond real-world labour productivity, even in highly automated manufacturing.

These figures are presented here because they appear in the source programme design, not because they have passed a plausibility check. The employment revenue line below should be read with that caveat attached.

Two ways to read this table. Without the employment stream, core platform revenue is $62.40T/yr, the food commitment is $37.44T/yr, and net profit is $13.93T/yr — figures that hold up against real-world benchmarks. With the employment stream included, every number in the total row is roughly 170× larger, and that entire multiplier traces back to the single flagged $48M/worker/year assumption above. The retrofit line ($67.43T/yr) does not carry the same flag — its underlying yield mechanics (10% on $306.5B) mirror the smart-city rental model already used elsewhere in this profile.
The Food Sovereignty commitment ($37.44T/yr) is larger than every other cost line combined, and is paid before net profit is calculated — the humanitarian benefit is a first-priority cost of doing business, not a discretionary allocation from what's left over.
