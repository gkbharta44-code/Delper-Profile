The Mission

Humanitarian by architecture, not by intention

Every commerce platform built before Delper has extracted value from the people at the base of the economic pyramid — charging them to transact, charging them to access, charging them to participate. Delper inverts this. The supply chain that has always taxed the consumer invisibly, the advertisers who have always profited from consumer attention, and the token traders who benefit from consumer-driven liquidity all pay into a single pool, returned as income to the person actually using the platform.

"The decision to charge the supply chain rather than the user is the founding humanitarian act. It cannot be removed without destroying the business model entirely — human welfare and commercial return are not in tension. They are the same variable."

Renewal, Not Abandonment

What happens to the city residents leave behind

When a population relocates to a completed smart city, the legacy city they leave is not abandoned. Delper reclaims and retrofits it — a second humanitarian commitment, run in one-for-one parity with the smart-city programme, so that renewal follows relocation everywhere it happens.

Capital Payback

How long until 4,400 cities pay for themselves

Total construction capital for 4,400 smart cities at $306.25B each: $1,347.5T. Two views of how long that takes to earn back, both using the realistic employment figure above.

Digital + Employment Only

Year 68

Using only the $10.62T/yr digital income stream plus employment revenue ramping to $12.83T/yr, cumulative revenue crosses $1,347.5T in Year 68 — 47 years past the Year-21 full build-out milestone.

Full Revenue Stack

Year 25

Adding smart-city rental income (7% yield) and supply chain fees — both already established elsewhere in this profile — cumulative revenue crosses $1,347.5T by Year 25, just 4 years past full build-out.

Rental income, not employment revenue, is what makes the 4,400-city programme payback-feasible within its own timeframe. This scenario models 4,400 smart cities specifically; the $306.5B retrofit programme above is a parallel, separately-financed track (25-year Medium Term Notes) and is not included in this payback calculation.

Financing Structure

Would a sovereign-guaranteed loan actually cover this?

A payback period isn't the same question as a loan's serviceability. A loan carries scheduled annual debt service — principal and interest — so what matters is whether yearly revenue covers that payment, not just whether cumulative revenue eventually exceeds total capital.

Why Extending the Tenor Doesn't Help

The shortfall is a revenue-level problem, not a timing problem

Even stripped of all interest, spreading $1,347.5T over 68 years still requires $19.82T/yr in principal alone. Digital + employment revenue doesn't cross that line until roughly city #3,153 of 4,400 — around Year 19 — leaving the first two decades of the build-out with no debt-service coverage under this revenue basis, regardless of tenor.

A Realistic Tenor, Once Rental Is Included

25 years has real-world precedent; 68 does not

Sovereign guarantees on infrastructure debt typically max out around 25–40 years, matching real concession periods. A 68-year guarantee would span 3–4 government administrations — a political-continuity risk most lenders would treat as unbankable on its own, independent of the underlying credit. The 25-year structure needs no such exception, and covers comfortably once rental income is counted.

The finding is consistent with the payback analysis above: rental income is what makes this programme financeable on realistic terms — not a longer guarantee period stretched to accommodate a narrower revenue base.
